With funding awarded by the institute of Physics I spent a week in Nice attending the MaNiTou summer school in gravitational waves, held at the university code d’azure. I spent a week between the lecture halls, observatory, and mediterranean sea, meeting and hearing from people who work on a huge variety of the research made possible through our measurements of gravitational waves, as well as those who made these measurements possible, and meaningful.
The experience has left me with some exciting new research ideas and directions that I would like to investigate further. The school was both challenging and enjoyable, and a great experience to consolidate the start my PhD and career in academia.
The school started with some familiar faces from BritGrav, the UK gravity conference we hosted in Cardiff this year, who I was able to catch up with over the first breakfast on campus before the lectures began! The morning focused on gravitational-wave data analysis, beginning with an introductory lecture followed by a hands-on session using real data. We worked through a Jupyter notebook analysing both injected signals and the famous GW150914 event detected by LIGO. We calculated the power spectral density (PSD), whitened and band-passed the data, applied tapering, and then transformed back to the time domain to identify the signal by eye. Although I have used these techniques before, it was particularly interesting that all of the functions were implemented from scratch rather than relying on packages such as GWpy. We also worked through an example of matched filtering using the GW150914 data, building directly on concepts introduced in the preceding lecture.
After lunch, we settled in for an afternoon of gravitational-wave theory. The lectures were heavily focused on general relativity and derived gravitational-wave waveforms from gauge theory. I found this one of the more challenging parts of the school. Even having studied a fair amount of GR previously, I think following the derivations in full would require being much closer to this area of research than I currently am. Nevertheless, it was fascinating to see how the waveform emerges from the properties of the metric. The lecturers had also provided a textbook, and I am planning to revisit this material alongside my notes.
One fun recommendation from the lecture was the app Phyphox, which allows you to use the sensors in your phone for physics experiments, including as an accelerometer!
The following morning the gravitational-wave theory lectures continued, introducing waveform modelling in more detail. We then heard an overview of the current landscape of gravitational-wave astronomy and the ongoing observing runs. We discussed how different gravitational-wave sources are classified according to both their signal properties and their astrophysical origins, and introduced the frequency ranges probed by the LVK detectors (ligo-virgo-kagra, the ground based interferometers which detect gravitational waves), LISA (which will be the first space-based gravitational-wave detector), and Pulsar Timing Arrays (a set of galactic pulsars which are monitored to search for correlations in their signatures). These themes reappeared throughout the rest of the week, making this lecture a useful overview of the field.
In the afternoon, we began a second block of data-analysis lectures focusing on Bayesian inference, which would later be consolidated through a practical notebook session. On the second day I was also able to put up the poster I had brought to the school. Bringing my work sparked several interesting conversations during the breaks we had during the day. People were particularly interested in how the simulations worked, and several attendees asked about specific applications, including neutron-star magnetic fields. This gave me a chance to discuss potential use cases and direct people towards the COMPAS documentation. I also had several interesting discussions about X-ray emission and binary evolution. A few people commented that they had not previously thought much about the evolutionary pathway leading to binary black-hole mergers, which made for some engaging conversations. 
The morning of day 3 focused on a practical application of Bayesian inference. We began with a simple Gaussian example before moving on to inference with gravitational-wave signals using the data we had prepared previously. Later in the morning we attended an introductory lecture on artificial intelligence. The applications discussed were not yet gravitational-wave specific, but several of the concepts overlapped with topics I had encountered while learning about simulation-based inference (SBI), a likelihood-free inference technique. I particularly enjoyed hearing about the different approaches through which neural networks can learn, especially generative models.
After this, we visited the Observatoire de la Côte d’Azur, starting with a lunch looking over the beautiful view of Nice! Around 300 people work at the observatory, and we were given a tour of both the site and its museum. We were able to see the historic telescope, which includes components designed by Eiffel, and several multimedia exhibits that used some very interesting projection techniques. During the tour, we also heard about research undertaken in Nice on developing early warning systems for seismic activity in the region, something I had not realised was a significant area of study there. We additionally had the opportunity to meet several PhD students working at the observatory and hear about their research.
The day concluded with a rather dramatic thunderstorm, which unfortunately brought an early end to our plans to swim in the sea!
The first lecture of the next day focused on gravitational-wave detectors. Rather than concentrating on the observational results, the lecture derived the required sensitivity of interferometers directly from the effect of gravitational waves on the experimental setup. This was a perspective I had not encountered before and found particularly interesting. We examined the operation of Michelson interferometers, discussing optical cavities, recycling mirrors, laser systems, and the various techniques used to suppress noise sources. As many attendees were on the data-analysis side of gravitational-wave astronomy, this provided a valuable insight into the experimental side of the field.
The following lecture introduced gravitational-wave cosmology and was one of the highlights of the week for me. We discussed methods for measuring the Hubble constant using gravitational-wave observations, making use of the luminosity distance information encoded in the signal. Different approaches for obtaining source redshifts were explored, including electromagnetic counterparts and the so-called "mass method", which uses population inference to relate observed masses to underlying source populations. This was a topic that generated a lot of discussion over lunch. I am particularly interested in exploring whether similar techniques could be applied to populations generated with COMPAS and compared with observational constraints.
In the afternoon, representatives from the three organising schools described their research programmes and involvement in gravitational-wave science. The final lecture covered the astrophysics of gravitational-wave sources, focusing on the evolutionary pathways that lead to compact binary mergers. Unsurprisingly, this was especially relevant to my own research interests and provided useful context for many of the discussions I had been having around my poster.
Friday, day 5, began with lectures on Pulsar Timing Arrays (PTAs). We first considered the "Metronome Timing Array", a tabletop analogue of a PTA, before extending the same principles to a network of pulsars. It was interesting to see how perturbations to spacetime can be detected through changes in signal arrival times across a distributed array.
The afternoon focused on future detector concepts. The first lecture introduced ground-based atomic interferometers, a technology I had not encountered previously. It was particularly exciting to learn that one of these facilities is currently being developed in the south of France. The final lecture of the day focused on LISA, the Laser Interferometer Space Antenna. Hearing about LISA is always exciting, and the lecture provided a realistic overview of both its scientific potential and the challenges that remain before launch. One point that particularly caught my attention was the suggestion that LISA analysis would rely heavily on Bayesian inference. Based on the examples discussed, I found myself wondering whether likelihood-free approaches such as SBI might also prove useful, and this is something I would like to explore further.
The final day began with a lecture on artificial-intelligence applications in gravitational-wave astronomy, focusing on the use of normalising flows for gravitational-wave data analysis. The closing lecture explored gravitational waves as probes of ultra-dense matter. This focused on what observations of compact objects might reveal about the possible structures of matter at extreme densities and how this could inform our understanding of fundamental physics.We also learned that next year's summer school will be held over a full month in Les Houches!
Overall, I thoroughly enjoyed attending the MaNiTou Summer School and found it an extremely valuable experience. The programme covered a remarkably broad range of topics, from gravitational-wave theory and detector physics to cosmology, data analysis, artificial intelligence, and source astrophysics. This variety meant that I was constantly exposed to new ideas and perspectives, while also deepening my understanding of areas more directly connected to my own research.
The lecturers also provided a textbook written specifically for the school, which will be an excellent resource for revisiting many of the topics that I would like to explore in more depth.
Beyond the scientific content, the school provided an excellent opportunity to meet students and
researchers from across the gravitational-wave community. Through both the organised activities and discussions around my poster, I was able to make a number of new connections that I expect to encounter again at future conferences and, potentially, through future collaborations. This, combined with the beautiful settings of the south of France, nightly swims in the Mediterranean Sea, and some delicious food, made the summer school a fantastic and valuable week!
